I am a Postdoctoral Associate in the Department of Psychiatry at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, working in the Carolina Center for Neurostimulation and the Frohlich Lab. My research is centered on biomimetic neuromodulation: developing brain stimulation signals that more closely reflect endogenous brain activity, and using human electrophysiology to optimize how those signals engage dysfunction-relevant circuits in depression.

My current work spans both mechanistic and translational psychiatry. I develop and help lead clinical trials testing personalized neuromodulation approaches, including closed-loop tACS in major depressive disorder, naturalistic stimulation approaches in antenatal depression, and endogenous waveform-based stimulation frameworks such as transcranial endogenous current stimulation (tECS). More broadly, I am interested in why brain stimulation so often relies on highly synthetic signals that do not resemble real neural activity, and whether treatment response can be improved by instead using physiologically grounded, biomimetic stimulation signals. This focus on endogenous waveform design and electrophysiological target engagement is the core of the independent translational program I am building. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

A major emphasis of my work is the use of electrophysiology, particularly high-density EEG, to define biomarkers of brain network dysfunction and quantify target engagement. I am especially interested in how oscillatory and non-oscillatory neural dynamics can be used not only to personalize stimulation, but to guide more rational and mechanistically informed intervention design. At UNC, I have developed biomimetic neuromodulation approaches including tECS and physiology-informed TMS (piTMS), while contributing to translational neuromodulation studies in depression and intracranial electrophysiology projects. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Before moving to UNC, I completed my PhD in Clinical Neuroscience at the University of Queensland Child Health Research Centre in Australia. My doctoral work investigated the use of non-invasive brain stimulation to improve attention in children following acquired brain injury. Across this work, I conducted structural and functional brain connectivity analyses, studied biomarkers of stimulation response, and developed and led the first remotely supervised at-home clinical trial of brain stimulation in pediatric acquired brain injury. This training gave me hands-on experience connecting mechanistic neuroscience, biomarker development, trial design, and clinical translation. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5} :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Across both pediatric and adult populations, I have worked with tDCS, tACS, rTMS, and HD-EEG in neurological and psychiatric settings. My background sits at the intersection of clinical neuroscience, engineering, and psychology, and I strongly value multidisciplinary integration. Guided in part by my MPH training, I also care about the broader questions that shape translation in the real world, including scalability, accessibility, implementation, and science communication. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7} :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

My long-term goal is to build an independent translational research program that uses human electrophysiology to design and optimize stimulation signals that more precisely engage depression-relevant circuits. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
